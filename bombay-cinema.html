Bombay Cinema refers to the Hindi-language film industry based in Mumbai, India. It began in 1913, with silent films such as Raja Harishchandra, which incorporated intertitles in Marathi, Hindi, and English. These early films were heavily influenced by colonization. Early Indian films were characterized by Western cinematic techniques and indigenous storytelling traditions. Under colonial rule, films served as a form of resistance, an assertion of cultural identity.

Nanda was often cast as demure; with her roles depicting vulnerability, emotional sensitivity, and moral purity. Her characters emphasizes the ideal feminine virtues of the 1950s and 1960s, and the early post-independence era.

Saira Banu was another mainstay of 1960s Bollywood. She portrayed charming, glamorous, and youthful characters. While typically lighthearted, her roles were still deeply rooted in traditional morality, and the aspirational feminity of 1960s Bombay cinema.

Vyjayanthimala starred in both Bombay cinema as well as Tamil films. Depicted as elegant, and known for her classical dance talents, she blended traditional Indian ideals with modern cinema. Her roles were highly cultural throughout here career spanning from the 1950s-1960s.

Rekha gained popularity in the 1970s, depicted as sensuous, complex, and independent. She went between the virtuous heroine and a bold, modern woman. Even today, she represents the shift to female autonomy and sexuality.
